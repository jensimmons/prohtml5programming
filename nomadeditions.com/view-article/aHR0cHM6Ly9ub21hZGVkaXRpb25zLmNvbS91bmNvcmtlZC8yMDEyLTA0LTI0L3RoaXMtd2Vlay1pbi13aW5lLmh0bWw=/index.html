Uncorked April 18, 2012

Tribes Roll the Dice

Wine has always been a gamble, as vineyard owners and producers know only too well. Frost, drought, heat, marketing trends, a crashing economy — those are only a few of the hazards. So it makes sense that a casino-owning Native American tribe, the Dry Creek Pomos of Sonoma and Mendocino Counties, is willing to take a risk on premium vino. The tribe has shelled out $24 million for 310 acres in the Alexander Valley right next to its River Rock Casino. And last week, the Lytton Pomos finalized their $13.3 million purchase of almost 270 acres from Jordan Winery. They've named their new vineyard “Bellacana,” a blend of the Italian word for “lovely” with the Pomo word for “river.” If the high-end wines they plan to make are as lilting as the name, they’ll hit three cab grapes on the slot machine and win the jackpot.

Just a Sip’ll Do You

Tim Bucher of Tastingroom.com, thinks more wineries should sell samples — 1.7 ounce mini bottles, to be exact, or a very small pour. After all, you get to test drive cars and scarf down food samples in grocery stores, so why not sip a preview of that $50 wine? Bucher’s website offers sampler flights prepared by chefs Mario Batali and Michael Chiarello, and wine personality Gary Vaynerchuk. Most six-bottle minipaks cost $30, which works out to about $120 for each star-studded 750 ml. bottle. Maybe they should name the samplers “Golden Sips”?

Dinner Theater for Foodies

Chicago super chef Rick Bayless keeps all three of his restaurants packed with delighted diners. And to entertain them he’s co-produced a play with Lookingglass Theater (co-founded by David Schwimmer of Friends fame) called Cascabel. Bayless plays a chef who wins the heart of a sad inn owner with his enchanted cooking. This new take on dinner theater has the audience eating the same foods that Bayless is whipping up onstage at $250 a head. The audience gets to drink wines, a major advantage over the actors. The event features three New Zealand sauvignon blancs (two by Kim Crawford) , a pair of syrahs and a 2008 Clos du Bois Merlot. It all leaves us wondering: Is the play the thing, or simply an aperitif?

Just the Thing for Chocoholics

Andrew Browne, CEO of Washington state-based Precept Wine, looked at the sales figures for chocolate wine (yes, it exists) in the US and decided something new was needed, namely a slightly less caloric version, sans the cream found in other blends. So last February his Chocolate Shop introduced a straight-up Bordeaux blend from California mixed with sugar and, well, cocoa. It tastes like… melted Hershey Bars with an attitude, but it’s selling briskly. What’s next? Twinkie-laced chardonnay?
